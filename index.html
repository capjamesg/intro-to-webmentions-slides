The problem: Silos own our data.

Silos control how our posts interact with other sites, limit the medium through which we can publish content (i.e. exclusively video, short-form text), and decide how to show our content.

But the web doesn't have to be this way.

What is Webmention?

Webmention is a simple way to notify any URL when you link to it. Site owners can display Webmentions to show likes, comments, bookmarks, and more.

Webmention is a standard, so it works on any site that complies with the specification.

By sending mentions, you can post content on your site that responds to posts on someone else's site.

First, sign up to webmention.io.

Then, add this code to the <head> of your site:


						<link
							rel="webmention"

  2. Send a mention to codename.lol using Telegraph.
(Demo)

When you send a mention, a tool like Telegraph:

  1. Discovers a Webmention endpoint for a site.
  2. Sends a POST request to the endpoint with the URL of (i) your post and (ii) the post you want to respond to.

Yep, sending a mention is just a POST request! üôå

Then, a site's Webmention endpoint will:

  1. Process and validate the mention.
  2. Do something with the mention (store it, send a webhook, etc.)

Why go to all of this effort?

You own your content and how it is displayed.

Webmention embodies a principle called POSSE.

Publish on your own site, syndicate elsewhere.

POSSE lets you own the original copy of your content and syndicate it elsewhere.

If you syndicate original content, you can choose how to display it on your site.

But... what can I do with mentions aside from post a comment? ü§î

You could...

  • Send a comment to someone's website
  • Post a like or a bookmark
  • Vote on a poll
  • RSVP to a post
  • And so much more...

Many of these ideas are enabled by small pieces of markup (microformats) added to links.

  • u-in-reply-to: Reply
  • u-like-of: Like
  • u-bookmark-of: Bookmark
  • p-rsvp: RSVP
